At the core of the new analytics movement is a stat called CORSI.

CORSI is a new way to track shots. In the past we’ve just been looking at shots on goal, CORSI is tracking total shot attempts.

CORSI correlates with winning more than any other stat. 75% of teams in the top 10 CORSI for the past 10 years have made the playoffs.

Why do people care about hockey analytics?

     The purpose of analytics is to figure out what events cause an outcome to happen what the best way is to make that outcome happens again. Finding correlations between these events is extremely important.

Correlation is the relationship between two concepts. In this case, its a team's CORSI% (don't worry about what CORSI is right now) and their chances of winning. As you can see, the Red team has a much higher CORSI for%, thus a higher winning %. Watch what happens if we lower their CORSI for% to just over 50%.

Not only does The Red Team move close to the 50% for winning chances, the Blue Team does as well. There is a very close relationship between these two stats. Because the stats are from two opposing teams, they will always being moving with each other. This is as extreme as the two stats can go.

You can see that the two teams, Red and blue, have an extremely high correlation. The must add up to 100%, so when one goes up, the other must come down.

     For example, there is a high correlation between scoring more goals than your opponent and winning. Even if that seems obvious, its an important relationship to make, because you can then start to look at bigger questions, like “what allows my team to score goals?” and “how can I stop the other team from scoring goals?”.
The concept of repeatability is incredibly important for any type of analytics. Goals themelves are not a great example of a repeatable metric, because they rely on many factors like shot location, quality of shot, defender’s position, goalie’s position, goalie’s strengths and weaknesses.

Repeatability is a measure of the effectiveness of a statistic. For a statistic (or a study) to be considered good, it should be able to be replicated with extremely similar results. For example, the sun setting in the west is a repeatable event, its all but guaranteed to happen every day. Thus, it’d have a repeatability of 1 (the highest it can be, from a 0-1 scale).

In the example above, we are measuring a coin flipping 2,000 times and seeing how many times it lands on heads, and how many times it lands on tails. Because the ratio of heads to tails will vary, this won’t have a repeatability of 1, but 95% of the time it will end up between 900-1100 heads, which gives it a repeatability of .9.

You can end up with extreme outliers, like 750 heads and 1250 tails, are less than 1 in 5,000. So the point of measuring repeatability isn't about if an event can happen, but how likely it is to happen.
     However a metric like shot attempts (better known as CORSI, but we will be getting more into that in Shot Metrics and Rate Stats) is much more repeatable because it relies on less separate factors, although those factors still do exist).
     Sports analytics have been a rapidly growing field for decades, especially now in the public sphere. More people having more access to open data has led to some tremendous innovations in sports analytics, some of which have begun to trickle its way into professional sports leagues.

Combating
Perception

There’s been a long standing battle in player evaluation between the “eye test” and metric based analytics.

The eye test is what it sounds like, how good a player appears to be from just watching him. The value of a person’s eye test very much depends on how knowledgeable and trustworthy a person’s hockey IQ is.

Metric based analytics track measurable things like what happens on the ice, how many shots for a player is on ice for, how often a player has the puck on his stick and, more recently, how a player exits the defensive zones and enters offensive zones.

Critics of the eye test point out that biases, like being the fan of a team or lucky scoring streaks, can heavily influence a person’s eye test. The eye test also tends to miss what happens away from the play, like positioning that might not be captured by tv cameras.

Critics of analytics often blame analysts for putting too much into numbers, not needing to watch games to evaluate and through talk of unquantifiables, like leadership, strength and grit.

Whats the most effective way of evaluating players? Like most things, it lies somewhere in the middle of everything else. Analytics are extremely helpful for cancelling out biases and evaluating smaller things that may not be initially noted on viewing.

Watching a player play is incredibly important to understanding their style. You can infer parts of their game from which metrics they succeed in, but you cannot know how smooth a skater is from their CORSI%.
